Graduate Research

Runoff Efficiency

At the watershed scale, rivers reflect the conversion of precipitation into surface runoff (Runoff Efficiency). The water balance of a catchment provides the discharge necessary for smaller scale processes to occur, including sediment transport, and influences channel size sometimes through catastrophic floods and often through smaller, repeated channel molding events. As climate changes, the water balance in many watersheds may be fundamentally altered, as has been observed in the Colorado River Basin. Even in more humid regions, many watersheds also show historical temperature sensitivity.

Discharge - Drainage Area Scaling

By altering discharge, runoff efficiency also influences the scaling of discharge with drainage area and potentially river channel size. In regions of low runoff efficiecny, discharge scales in a non-linear way. These low runoff efficiency regions may have channels shaped by more infrequent discharges compared to regions where the common assumption of a linear relationship between discharge and drainage area holds (Erikson et al. 2024).

Sediment Transport

At the grain scale, sediment transport is contingent on stresses generated near the bed surface and is influenced by surrounding particles. In some cases, when finer-grained sediment is added to an existing coarser material, transport inceases. Other times, adding sediment only results in burial. On a regulated river with large sediment supply variation, adding sand to cobbles did not change the mean stress required for cobble transport, but it did change the standard deviation.

Alluvial River Channel Form

At the reach scale, the step-pool morphology has many proposed explanations for its formation, but quantitative theory is limited. One category of explanations suggest that steps form where there are rough patches that cause preferentially disentrainment. This idea can be modeled by introducing roughness variation into a coupled system of Saint Venant - Exner equations.

Arctic Rivers

Rivers in permafrost regions are experiencing rapidly rising temperatures, which are likely to leave a distinct signature on the landscape. Working with Evan Dethier, we have been investigating thaw slumps and hypothesizing their role in altering coupled hillslope-channel processes using image segmentation and remote sensing.

Undergraduate Research

As an undergraduate, I worked with Julia Carr and Roman DiBiase examining coarse, bedrock rivers in Taiwan. I measured grain size and channel bed roughness using data generated through drone surveys. I also was able to join Al Neely on a trip to Yucaipa Ridge in California to survey the hillslopes after the 2020 El Dorado fire.
